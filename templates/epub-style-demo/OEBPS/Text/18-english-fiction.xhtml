I. The Clock in the Hall

The rain had ended before breakfast, leaving the street bright and narrow. From the window, Clara could see a line of wet roofs, a delivery cart, and the old clock above the station door.

A clerk arrived at the station door with a lantern in one hand and a folded notice in the other. Clara watched the large black minute hand tremble, then settle, as if the clock had been listening to every step on the platform.

She had meant to stay indoors and sort the letters on her desk, but the morning had the clear, washed look that always made errands feel important. She put the smallest letter in her pocket and went downstairs.

"If anyone asks for me," she said, "tell them I have gone to see whether the clock is right."

The hall was quiet. Her boots made a small sound on the tiles, and the sound followed her all the way to the front step. Outside, every puddle held a piece of sky.

At the corner she stopped to read the letter again. It was only three lines long, written in a careful hand, and signed with a name she did not know.

Come at nine. Bring the blue key. Do not trust the clock.

That was all. Yet it seemed enough to change the shape of the morning. The station clock struck once, although the hour was still early, and a flock of birds lifted from the telegraph wires.
